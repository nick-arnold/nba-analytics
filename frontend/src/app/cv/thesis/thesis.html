Automation and Evaluation of Graduated Dot Maps

Nicholas D. Arnold, Bernhard Jenny & Denis White

Citation

Arnold, N. D., Jenny, B., & White, D. (2017). Automation and evaluation of graduated dot maps. International Journal of Geographical Information Science, 31(12), 2524–2542.

doi.org/10.1080/13658816.2017.1359747 →

The problem with dot maps

Dot maps are one of the oldest and most intuitive tools in cartography — place a dot for every unit of something (people, cows, bushels of corn) and let the reader perceive density visually. But when you try to automate dot map generation in GIS software, a stubborn problem emerges: where exactly do you put each dot?

The dominant approach is pseudo-random placement — scatter dots randomly within each geographic enumeration area (a county, a census tract, a country). It's fast and simple, but the results are often poor. Dense areas end up with hundreds of overlapping dots stacked on top of each other, forming dark blobs that tell the reader nothing useful. Sparse areas look fine. The map becomes simultaneously overloaded and unreadable in the places that matter most.

The idea behind graduated dot maps

The central insight of this research is straightforward: instead of using many small same-size dots, use fewer dots of varying sizes. A county with ten times the population of its neighbor doesn't need ten times the dots — it needs a smaller number of larger dots that together convey a proportionally greater quantity.

This is the graduated dot map. The technique had been used manually by skilled cartographers for decades, but no one had built a reliable automated method for generating them. That's the gap this research fills.

The method

The automated algorithm works in two stages. First, it places dots using blue-noise patterns — a distribution strategy borrowed from computer graphics that spaces dots evenly without the rigid regularity of a grid. Blue-noise placement looks natural to the human eye and eliminates the accidental clustering that plagues pseudo-random methods.

Second, a clustering algorithm scans the placed dots and identifies groups that are too dense to read comfortably. It merges those groups into single dots of larger size, preserving the quantitative information while dramatically reducing visual clutter. The result is a map where every dot is legible and the reader can actually estimate values rather than just perceiving a vague gradient of darkness.

What the study found

To test whether any of this actually made a difference for map readers, a user study was conducted with nearly 300 participants. Four map types were compared head-to-head:

  • Graduated dot maps (the new method)
  • Pseudo-random dot maps (the standard automated approach)
  • Blue-noise dot maps (random placement replaced with blue-noise, but no size graduation)
  • Proportional circle maps (a common alternative to dot maps)

Participants were asked to estimate values from each map type. Graduated dot maps produced significantly more accurate estimates than the other three types. Participants also expressed a clear preference for graduated dot maps — they were easier to read and less visually overwhelming.

The accuracy advantage is likely explained by the reduced dot count. When an enumeration area contains 12 dots instead of 120, the reader can actually count or group them. The cognitive load drops dramatically and estimation improves.

Why it matters

Most GIS software — including ArcGIS and QGIS — still defaults to pseudo-random dot placement when generating dot density maps. The tools that cartographers rely on every day produce maps that are demonstrably harder to read than they need to be.

This research provides both the algorithmic foundation for a better approach and the empirical evidence that it works. The combination of blue-noise placement and size-based clustering is something that could be implemented as a standard option in any mapping software. The fact that it hasn't been yet is mostly a matter of inertia — the pseudo-random method is decades old and deeply embedded in existing workflows.

The work also received recognition beyond academia — the graduated dot map technique is discussed in a standalone section (§19.4, pp. 352–353) of the fourth edition of Thematic Cartography and Geovisualization by Slocum et al. (2022), one of the standard textbooks in the field.

Abstract

Dot mapping is a traditional method for visualizing quantitative data, but current automated dot mapping techniques are limited. The most common automated method places dots pseudo-randomly within enumeration areas, which can result in overlapping dots and very dense dot clusters for areas with large values. These issues affect users' ability to estimate values. Graduated dot maps use dots with different sizes that represent different values. With graduated dot maps the number of dots on a map is smaller, reducing the likelihood of overlapping dots. This research introduces an automated method of generating graduated dot maps that arranges dots with blue-noise patterns to avoid overlap and uses clustering algorithms to replace densely packed dots with those of larger sizes. A user study comparing graduated dot maps, pseudo-random dot maps, blue-noise dot maps and proportional circle maps with almost 300 participants was conducted. Results indicate that map users can more accurately extract values from graduated dot maps than from the other map types. This is likely due to the smaller number of dots per enumeration area in graduated dot maps. Map users also appear to prefer graduated dot maps over other map types.
